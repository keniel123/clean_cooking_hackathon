Proposed concept

Use AI to shift clean cooking from appliance pilots to grid-aware adoption at scale.

GridCook AI combines smart-plug e-cooker data, mini-grid telemetry, clean-cooking access indicators, learned cooker profiles, and household preference signals to forecast cooking load, recommend clean-cooking windows, and help operators deploy electric cooking without creating evening peaks or trust problems. A daytime rewards layer gives households bill credits or vouchers for cooking when solar and battery headroom are strongest, while a health proxy estimates whether e-cooking is actually replacing smoky fuel use.

What We Use AI For

The AI layer is practical: it reduces implementation risk by turning raw telemetry into action for households, mini-grid operators, and funders.

Profile

Household and appliance habit learning

Inputs
Verified sessions, start hour, kWh, duration, peak watts, reward history, optional survey preferences.
Model
Feature aggregation plus simple clustering into daytime-ready, evening-anchored, high-energy, low-use, and irregular profiles.
Output
Anonymized cooker profiles used for realistic nudges, reward targeting, and grid-headroom planning.
Forecast

Cooking and load prediction

Inputs
Smart-plug power, time of day, household profile, PV and battery telemetry.
Model
Short-horizon demand forecast plus session clustering.
Output
Expected cooking load by hour and likely peak stress periods.
Optimize

Clean-cooking window recommender

Inputs
Battery SOC, PV forecast, phase load, voltage history, meal-time preferences.
Model
Constraint-based scheduler with user opt-out and fairness limits.
Output
SMS/WhatsApp prompts and operator-safe staggered e-cooking windows.
Target

Adoption and subsidy prioritization

Inputs
Clean-cooking gap, electricity readiness, household usage, affordability signals.
Model
Propensity and equity scoring, audited against rural/low-income bias.
Output
Which households or villages should receive appliance finance first.
Health

Smoke-exposure proxy and fuel-stacking monitor

Inputs
E-cooker sessions, household fuel survey, cooking location, ventilation, optional health-diary check-ins.
Model
Rules-based risk score plus classifier for replacement versus stacking behavior.
Output
Estimated avoided smoky cooking sessions, health-priority support tier, and CHW follow-up list.
Detect

Appliance and grid anomaly detection

Inputs
Voltage, current, power spikes, connection gaps, temperature where available.
Model
Unsupervised anomaly detection and threshold rules for safety.
Output
Technician alerts before unsafe wiring or overload becomes a failure case.

Fuel-stacking and impact monitoring

Inputs
E-cooker kWh, survey responses, fuel purchases, cooking frequency.
Model
Adoption classifier and before/after impact estimator.
Output
Evidence for funders: clean-cooking usage, avoided biomass, and time savings.
Explain

Operator and funder copilot

Inputs
Dashboard metrics, deployment notes, incidents, household feedback.
Model
Retrieval over pilot data and structured proposal logic.
Output
Weekly briefs, risk summaries, and Clean Cooking Alliance proposal drafts.

AI role

The model forecasts green windows, estimates likely cooking demand, recommends nudges, and tunes rewards so the mini-grid shifts load without overpaying.

ForecastPredict when households are likely to cook.
Price signalIncrease rewards when daytime solar is underused.
FairnessBoost households that had fewer green-window opportunities.

Guardrails

Rewards must be voluntary, privacy-preserving, capped, and linked to plausible cooking sessions so the system does not create new risks.

VerifyReward real e-cooker power shapes, not random plug usage.
CapLimit points per household per day to reduce gaming.
Opt outNever penalize households that must cook at night.

Robustness and Failure Cases

These are the points judges will challenge; the concept should acknowledge them upfront.

Affordability and fuel stacking

Users may keep using biomass if e-cooking feels expensive or unreliable. Mitigation: lease-to-own appliances, tariff incentives, starter recipe support, and usage-based subsidy targeting.

Grid headroom and evening peaks

Cooking can create coincident demand when batteries are lower. Mitigation: SOC-aware scheduling, phase balancing, opt-in cooking windows, and hard safety constraints.

Connectivity and trust

Low-bandwidth sites cannot depend on always-on apps. Mitigation: offline-first rules, SMS/WhatsApp prompts, local champions, and manual override.

Model bias and weak labels

Access data is modeled and household outcomes require surveys. Mitigation: report uncertainty, validate with field data, and avoid using AI scores as automatic exclusion rules.

Health claims need field validation

The dashboard estimates reduced smoke-exposure sessions, not disease reduction. Mitigation: add PM2.5/CO sensors, fuel-use surveys, CHW review, and clear consent in the pilot.
